## DRAFT — Essay 1: Lightweight research with rigor

At John Deere, I built the PM-UX Playbook — a lightweight research scaffold that let product managers run their own discovery and validation cycles without sacrificing rigor. It worked on three principles I'd carry into Khan Academy.

**Methodological guardrails over methodological gatekeeping.** Rather than acting as a bottleneck for every study, I authored decision trees that mapped question type to method (e.g., a comprehension question → 5-task usability test, n=8; a preference question → max-diff survey, n=80). Teams could move fast because the right approach was pre-vetted; they didn't need my approval for the design, only my review of the analysis.

**Pre-built starter kits with ethical defaults baked in.** The self-serve survey platform I shipped for 250+ products embedded informed-consent language, fatigue caps, recruitment-bias checks, and accessibility minimums by default. Teams couldn't skip them, but they also didn't have to think about them — the scaffolding did the work. Setup time dropped 60% and completion rates rose 25%, while the floor of methodological quality went up, not down.

**Office-hours synthesis, not solo synthesis.** I held weekly insight reviews where teams brought their raw findings and I helped them interrogate the data — pushing back on small-n claims, surfacing alternative explanations, validating that the recommendation followed from the evidence. It scaled because I wasn't doing the research; I was teaching teams to read their own. PM UX literacy lifted 80% across the India teams over a year.

For Khan Academy specifically, I'd extend this with AI-assisted thematic synthesis (Dovetail + LLM tagging) where the model proposes a coding scheme from interview transcripts and I review for bias, inappropriate inference, and missed nuance — Human-in-the-Loop checkpoints exactly where the methodological risk lives. The lightweight part is that teams get directional answers in days instead of weeks; the rigor part is that the evidence chain remains auditable, the ethical defaults are enforced upstream, and a senior researcher signs off before any insight becomes a product decision.

## DRAFT — Essay 2: AI-enhanced research workflow with human-in-the-loop

At the American Board of Internal Medicine (ABIM), I'm architecting a research workflow that uses fine-tuned transformers (ClinicalBERT, RoBERTa) to surface communication patterns across 985 patient–physician conversations — a corpus that would take a team of researchers months to code by hand. The pipeline operationalizes two evidence-based clinical-communication frameworks (Calgary–Cambridge and NURSE) into 11 measurable constructs. The human-in-the-loop architecture has four checkpoints, each placed where the epistemic risk actually sits.

**Checkpoint 1 — Annotation taxonomy review (before the model runs).** Before any AI involvement, I designed the gold-standard annotation taxonomy in Label Studio with explicit inter-rater reliability protocols. Two human annotators independently code a stratified sample; we don't proceed until Cohen's kappa clears clinical-audit thresholds. The model never trains on a label dictionary that humans haven't first agreed on.

**Checkpoint 2 — Synthetic-data leakage gate.** I built automated validation that detects PHI leakage in any synthetic data used for augmentation, plus a human review step before any new synthetic record enters the training set. Responsible AI isn't a policy doc here — it's an executable check.

**Checkpoint 3 — Confidence-banded human review of model output.** The classifier doesn't auto-assign labels. Below a calibrated confidence threshold, predictions go to a research-coordinator review queue; only above-threshold predictions flow downstream. The human-in-the-loop position is precisely where the methodological risk lives — ambiguous cases — not where the model is already confident.

**Checkpoint 4 — Bias-detection layer on outputs.** I extended the pipeline with a four-label bias-detection framework that flags communication disparities (e.g., differential interruption rates by patient demographic). The detector itself gets human-spot-checked weekly; we don't trust a bias detector to self-validate.

The result is a research workflow where AI compresses what would be six months of manual coding into weeks, but every methodological decision and every contested case still has a human signature on it. That's the architecture I'd bring to scaling Khan Academy's research operations: AI for throughput, humans for judgment, with checkpoint placement determined by where the actual epistemic risk sits — not where the technology is most impressive.
