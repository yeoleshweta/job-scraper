## Screening / Essay Questions

### Why are you interested in BetterHelp and this role?

BetterHelp is doing user research in one of the highest-stakes contexts that exists: helping people find the right therapist for some of the most sensitive moments of their lives. Doing that well — at the scale of 30,000+ therapists and millions of members — requires a researcher who treats rigor and ethics as inseparable. I do.

This role is also an unusually precise fit for my background. At John Deere, I was the sole UX researcher for the India product organization, embedded across 6+ product teams, with no playbook and no precedent. I built the research practice from scratch: a PM-UX Playbook that raised PM UX literacy by 80%, a self-serve survey platform deployed across 250+ products that cut setup time 60% and raised completion rates 25%, and a full library of research templates, standards, and case studies. That is exactly the mandate BetterHelp is describing for its first dedicated UX researcher. I've already done this job.

### Describe your mixed-methods research experience.

I've worked across the full spectrum of qualitative and quantitative methods throughout my career. On the qualitative side: contextual inquiry, semi-structured user interviews, usability testing (moderated and unmoderated), diary studies, card sorting, affinity mapping, and persona and journey map development. On the quantitative side: survey design and analysis, A/B and multivariate testing, behavioral analytics (Hotjar, FullStory, session recordings), heatmap analysis, segmentation, clustering, and statistical significance testing.

Some concrete examples of mixed-methods integration:
- At UrbanFit, I combined Hotjar behavioral analytics with moderated usability testing to redesign the wellness platform — the result was a 35% engagement lift, 28% bounce rate drop, and 2.5x newsletter sign-up increase.
- At Capita (Arcadia Group), I built behavioral cohorts and ran A/B experiments with 10% control methodology across 7 fashion brands, driving an 11% conversion lift.
- At John Deere, I ran longitudinal contextual inquiry and usability studies alongside survey research across 250+ products, generating insights that shaped quarterly product road maps.
- At ABIM (current role), I'm applying NLP and fine-tuned transformers (ClinicalBERT, RoBERTa) to 3,500+ clinical interaction records, establishing inter-rater reliability protocols and bias-detection frameworks for sensitive communication data — quantitative rigor in a deeply qualitative domain.

### Tell us about a time you built or scaled a research practice.

At John Deere, I joined as the sole UX researcher for the India product organization — a team of 6+ product squads with no existing research infrastructure, no templates, no shared understanding of what UX research was or why it mattered.

I started by mapping the landscape: stakeholder interviews with PMs and designers across all six teams to understand their decision-making processes and where research could add the most leverage. From there I built the foundation: a Research Ops framework with standardized templates for briefs, discussion guides, synthesis artifacts, and reports; a PM-UX Playbook that translated research literacy into the language PMs already used (metrics, sprint planning, road map prioritization); and a self-serve survey platform that any product team could use without involving a developer, reducing setup time 60% and raising completion rates 25%.

The outcome: research went from "nice to have" to a standing agenda item in quarterly planning. PM UX literacy increased by 80% as measured by self-assessment surveys before and after the Playbook rollout. And I moved from being the bottleneck to being the infrastructure — teams could run lightweight research autonomously, and I focused on the high-complexity studies that needed a senior researcher.

That is exactly what I would do at BetterHelp: build the infrastructure that makes research scale beyond one person.

### How do you approach research with vulnerable or sensitive user populations?

Thoughtfully and with explicit ethical design. My current role at ABIM has been the best training ground for this: I work with clinical communication data from physician-patient interactions, where the stakes of mishandling data — or of biased model outputs — are real and consequential. I've built bias-detection frameworks on 3,500+ clinical records, established inter-rater reliability protocols for human annotation, and designed data workflows to keep patient data out of AI model training.

The principles I apply to sensitive research contexts: informed consent that is genuinely comprehensible (not legalese); recruitment that does not exploit vulnerability or create undue pressure; study designs that allow participants to stop or skip without friction; synthesis that preserves the emotional nuance of qualitative data rather than reducing it to a bullet point; and findings that are reported with appropriate care about how they will be used.

At BetterHelp, this means approaching member research with the same care you'd want a researcher to bring to your own mental health journey — because that's what it is.
